# VocabBuilder — Design Context

## Users

**Primary audience**: Language learners at all levels, MVP targeting Vietnamese learners of English but built for global scale. These are people who want to acquire vocabulary naturally — not through rote memorization but through contextual encounters with real language.

**Usage context**: Daily, ambient use. During free moments — commutes, breaks, before bed. This is a *fill-the-gaps* app, not a *sit-down-and-study* app. Sessions are short (2-5 minutes of swiping quotes, quick lookups), but frequent. The app should reward brief visits and never feel like homework.

**Job to be done**: Encounter interesting ideas in English → look up unfamiliar phrases in context → absorb them through spaced repetition exercises that feel like reading, not drilling.

## Brand Personality

**Three words**: Quiet, literary, comfortable.

The app should feel like a well-made notebook — something you pick up because it's *pleasant*, not because it nags you. The learning happens almost as a side effect of reading things that are genuinely interesting.

**Emotional goals**:
- Comfort and ease while using — never overwhelmed
- The satisfaction of a beautiful reading experience
- Gentle intellectual stimulation, not gamified urgency

## Aesthetic Direction

**Visual tone**: Editorial minimalism with a literary soul. Think high-end independent publisher, not Silicon Valley SaaS. The typography does the heavy lifting. Decoration is organic and sparse, never synthetic.

**Primary reference**: [Ellipsus.com](https://ellipsus.com) — high-contrast monochrome, generous whitespace, serif-forward typography, smooth quiet animations, a sense of craft and humanity. The site feels like it was made by people who care about words.

**Anti-references**:
- ❌ Duolingo — too colorful, too gamified, too energetic
- ❌ Generic flashcard apps — sterile, utilitarian, no personality
- ❌ AI-aesthetic dark mode — cyan accents, purple gradients, neon glow
- ❌ Overly animated interfaces — bouncy, springy, attention-competing motion

**Theme**: Both light and dark, equally considered. Light mode is the primary reading experience (warm off-white, ink-black text). Dark mode should feel like reading under a lamp — warm, not clinical.

**Color palette direction**: Monochrome-dominant with warm undertones. Near-black and warm off-white as the foundation. Accent color should be restrained — used only for interactive affordances, never decorative. Tint neutrals warm (toward the paper/ink metaphor).

**Current fonts** (working well, keep refining):
- **Display/body**: Instrument Serif — italic for looked-up phrases, regular for quote text
- **UI/labels**: Inter — uppercase tracking for small labels, clean for buttons
- **Monospace**: Geist Mono — pronunciation, code-like elements

## Design Principles

1. **Typography carries the design.** The interface should be beautiful even without a single icon or illustration. Type scale, weight contrast, and serif/sans pairing create all the hierarchy needed.

2. **Quiet motion, not silence.** Animations exist to communicate state changes and provide tactile feedback — smooth reveals, gentle fades, elastic card swipes. Never bouncy, never competing for attention. One well-timed transition > ten micro-animations.

3. **Breathe.** Generous whitespace is not wasted space. Reading comfort demands rhythm: tight groupings within cards, generous separation between sections. Never cram.

4. **Implicit over explicit.** Match the learning philosophy — the interface teaches through gentle cues, not labels and instructions. Progressive disclosure. The interface feels simple on first use, but reveals depth through interaction.

5. **Craft, not chrome.** Every detail should feel intentional. No decorative elements that don't serve comprehension or comfort. If it doesn't help the reader read, question whether it belongs.
